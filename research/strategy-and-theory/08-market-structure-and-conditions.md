# Market Structure and Conditions

Understanding the broader market environment is the single most important factor in swing trading success. Studies consistently show that 60-80% of individual stocks follow the general market direction. Trading the best setups in the wrong market conditions leads to losses; mediocre setups in favorable conditions often produce gains. This document covers how to read, classify, and act on market structure and conditions.

---

## 1. Market Regimes

A market regime is the dominant behavioral state of the market over a given period. Correctly identifying the current regime determines which strategies to deploy, how aggressively to size positions, and whether to be long, short, or in cash.

### 1.1 The Three Primary Regimes

| Regime | Characteristics | Swing Trading Approach |
|--------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Bull (Uptrend)** | Higher highs & higher lows on indices; breadth expanding; most sectors rising | Buy pullbacks to support; trend-following entries; larger position sizes |
| **Bear (Downtrend)** | Lower highs & lower lows; breadth contracting; defensive sectors outperform | Short rallies into resistance; reduce position size; raise cash; tighten stops |
| **Sideways (Range)** | Price oscillates between support and resistance; no clear trend; choppy action | Fade extremes of the range; smaller positions; mean-reversion strategies |

### 1.2 Volatility Sub-Regimes

Each primary regime can occur in high or low volatility, creating six effective sub-regimes:

| Volatility | Bull Market | Bear Market | Sideways Market |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Low Vol** | Ideal for swing longs; steady grinds higher; low drawdown risk | Rare; usually a pause before acceleration lower | Tight ranges; small profits; best avoided |
| **High Vol** | Fast moves with sharp pullbacks; widen stops; reduce size | Capitulation selling; massive intraday swings; very dangerous for longs | Whipsaws; false breakouts; best to stand aside |

### 1.3 Tools to Identify the Current Regime

#### Moving Average Slopes

The slope and relationship of key moving averages reveal trend direction and strength:

- **20 EMA > 50 SMA > 200 SMA (all rising):** Strong bull regime. Buy pullbacks to the 20 EMA.
- **20 EMA < 50 SMA < 200 SMA (all falling):** Strong bear regime. Short rallies to the 20 EMA.
- **Moving averages flat and intertwined:** Sideways/transitional regime. Reduce activity.
- **Price above 200 SMA but below 50 SMA:** Corrective pullback in a longer-term uptrend. Watch for support at the 200 SMA.

**Practical check:** On the S&P 500 daily chart, compare the 20 EMA slope over the last 10 bars. If it is rising at an angle > 30 degrees visually, the short-term trend is strongly bullish. Flatten or decline indicates caution.

#### ADX (Average Directional Index)

ADX measures trend strength regardless of direction:

| ADX Value | Interpretation | Swing Approach |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| 0-15 | No trend; very weak | Avoid trend strategies; consider mean-reversion |
| 15-25 | Emerging trend | Watch for breakout confirmation |
| 25-40 | Strong trend | Primary swing trading zone; trend-following entries |
| 40-60 | Very strong trend | Ride existing positions; avoid counter-trend trades |
| 60+ | Extremely strong; rare | Possible exhaustion approaching; tighten trailing stops |

**Key nuance:** A rising ADX from below 20 signals a new trend is forming. A falling ADX from above 40 signals the trend is weakening, not necessarily reversing.

#### VIX (CBOE Volatility Index)

The VIX measures implied volatility of S&P 500 options and serves as a real-time fear gauge:

| VIX Level | Market Interpretation | Swing Implications |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 10-15 | Extreme complacency | Calm markets; tighter stops work; watch for volatility expansion |
| 15-20 | Normal/healthy | Standard swing conditions; full-size positions |
| 20-25 | Elevated uncertainty | Wider stops needed; reduce position size 25-50% |
| 25-35 | High fear | Bear market territory; sharp rallies and selloffs; nimble only |
| 35-50 | Panic | Capitulation possible; massive opportunity but extreme risk |
| 50+ | Crisis-level | Historic extremes (2008, 2020); stand aside or position for snap-back |

**VIX mean-reversion:** The VIX has a strong tendency to revert to its mean (~18-20 historically). Spikes above 35 often precede intermediate-term market lows by 1-4 weeks. A sustained VIX below 15 often precedes volatility expansion.

**VIX term structure:** When front-month VIX futures trade above back-month (backwardation), fear is acute and a market bounce is often near. When they trade below (contango), complacency reigns.

## 2. Market Structure

Market structure is the framework of price action that reveals who is in control (buyers or sellers) and where critical decision points lie.

### 2.1 Support and Resistance

Support and resistance are price zones where supply and demand concentrate.

#### Types of Support/Resistance

| Type | Description | Strength |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Horizontal S/R** | Previous swing highs/lows where price reversed | Strong; more touches = stronger |
| **Trendlines** | Diagonal lines connecting swing lows (support) or highs (resistance) | Moderate; requires 3+ touches for validity |
| **Moving Averages** | Dynamic S/R that moves with price (20, 50, 200 SMA/EMA) | Moderate to strong; institutionally watched |
| **VWAP** | Volume-weighted average price; key for intraday and short-term | Strong for day/multi-day trades |
| **Round Numbers** | Psychological levels ($50, $100, $200) | Moderate; more significant on large-cap stocks |
| **Gap Levels** | Unfilled gap highs and lows | Strong; gaps act as magnets |
| **Fibonacci Retracements** | 38.2%, 50%, 61.8% retracements of prior moves | Moderate; self-fulfilling when widely watched |
| **Volume Profile Nodes** | High-volume nodes (HVN) attract price; low-volume nodes (LVN) repel | Strong; reflects actual traded interest |

#### Principles of S/R

- **Role reversal:** Broken support becomes resistance and vice versa. This is one of the most reliable principles in technical analysis.
- **Zone, not line:** Support and resistance are zones (typically 1-3% wide), not exact prices. Place stops beyond the zone, not at it.
- **Confluence:** When multiple types of S/R overlap at the same price zone, the level is significantly stronger. Example: a 200 SMA that aligns with a horizontal support and a 61.8% Fibonacci retracement.
- **Time and touches:** A level that has held for months and been tested 3+ times is stronger than a recently formed one.
- **Volume confirmation:** High volume on a test of S/R that holds = strong level. Low volume breakdown through S/R = possible fake-out.

### 2.2 Trend Structure: Higher Highs / Higher Lows (HH/HL) and Lower Highs / Lower Lows (LH/LL)

Price action trend structure is the most fundamental framework for reading the market.

#### Uptrend Structure

```
        HH
       / \
      /   \
    HL     HH
   / \    / \
  /   \  /   \
HL    HL
```

- **Definition:** Successive higher highs (HH) and higher lows (HL).
- **Trend intact as long as:** Each pullback low stays above the prior pullback low.
- **First warning sign:** Price makes a lower high (LH) instead of a new HH.
- **Trend break:** Price makes a lower low (LL) below the most recent HL. This is the structural shift.

#### Downtrend Structure

```
LL
 \   LL
  \ / \
   LH  \
        LH
       / \
      /   LL
     LH
```

- **Definition:** Successive lower highs (LH) and lower lows (LL).
- **Trend intact as long as:** Each rally high stays below the prior rally high.
- **First warning sign:** Price makes a higher low (HL) instead of a new LL.
- **Trend break:** Price makes a higher high (HH) above the most recent LH.

#### Break of Structure (BOS) and Change of Character (CHoCH)

These terms from ICT/Smart Money Concepts are widely used:

- **BOS (Break of Structure):** Price continues its trend by breaking the last significant swing high (in uptrend) or swing low (in downtrend). This confirms trend continuation.
- **CHoCH (Change of Character):** The first structural break in the opposite direction. In an uptrend, this is the first lower low; in a downtrend, the first higher high. This signals a potential trend reversal.

**Practical application for swing traders:**
1. Identify the trend structure on the daily chart (HH/HL or LH/LL).
2. In an uptrend, enter long at each HL with a stop below it.
3. If a CHoCH occurs (price breaks below the last HL), close longs and wait for clarity.
4. After a CHoCH, watch for the new trend structure to establish before entering the new direction.

### 2.3 Wyckoff Market Cycles

Richard Wyckoff's framework describes the four phases of any market cycle. It remains one of the most powerful models for understanding institutional behavior.

#### The Four Phases

| Phase | Description | Duration | Volume Signature | Swing Strategy |
|-------|-------------|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Accumulation** | Smart money quietly buys after a downtrend; price trades in a range | Weeks to months | Declining on selloffs; increasing on rallies | Watch for spring (false breakdown); prepare long positions |
| **Markup** | Demand exceeds supply; price trends upward with healthy pullbacks | Months | Increasing on up moves; declining on pullbacks | Primary swing long zone; buy pullbacks |
| **Distribution** | Smart money sells into strength; price trades in a range at highs | Weeks to months | Increasing on selloffs; declining on rallies | Take profits on longs; watch for UTAD (upthrust after distribution) |
| **Markdown** | Supply exceeds demand; price trends downward | Months | Increasing on down moves; declining on rallies | Short rallies or stay in cash |

#### Key Wyckoff Events

**Accumulation Events:**
- **PS (Preliminary Support):** First significant buying after a decline. Volume increases but the decline is not yet over.
- **SC (Selling Climax):** High-volume panic selling that marks the approximate low. Often a long lower wick.
- **AR (Automatic Rally):** Snap-back rally after the SC. Defines the upper boundary of the accumulation range.
- **ST (Secondary Test):** Price retests the SC low on lower volume, confirming demand.
- **Spring:** A false breakdown below the SC/ST lows that shakes out weak hands. The highest-probability long entry in the Wyckoff model. Volume should be low on the spring and then expand as price re-enters the range.
- **SOS (Sign of Strength):** A rally on strong volume that breaks above the trading range. Confirms the markup phase is beginning.
- **LPS (Last Point of Support):** A pullback after the SOS that holds above the previous resistance (now support). Excellent swing entry point.

**Distribution Events:**
- **PSY (Preliminary Supply):** First significant selling after a rally.
- **BC (Buying Climax):** High-volume euphoric buying that marks the approximate high.
- **AR (Automatic Reaction):** Selloff after the BC that defines the lower boundary.
- **ST (Secondary Test):** Price retests the BC high on lower volume.
- **UTAD (Upthrust After Distribution):** False breakout above the BC high that traps late buyers. Analogous to the spring in accumulation.
- **SOW (Sign of Weakness):** A decline on strong volume that breaks below the trading range.
- **LPSY (Last Point of Supply):** A feeble rally after the SOW. Good short entry.

### 2.4 Order Flow Basics

Order flow analysis looks at the actual transactions occurring in the market rather than just the price chart. While primarily used by day traders, swing traders benefit from understanding the concepts.

#### Key Concepts

- **Bid/Ask:** The bid is the highest price buyers are willing to pay; the ask is the lowest price sellers are willing to accept. The spread between them reflects liquidity.
- **Market orders vs. limit orders:** Market orders execute immediately at the best available price (aggressive). Limit orders sit on the book waiting to be filled (passive). Aggressive buyers (market buy orders hitting the ask) drive price up.
- **Volume Delta:** The difference between aggressive buying volume and aggressive selling volume. Positive delta = more aggressive buyers. Persistent positive delta at support confirms demand.
- **Order book imbalance:** Large resting orders on the bid side (support) or ask side (resistance) can act as walls. However, these can be spoofed (placed and canceled to manipulate perception).

#### What Swing Traders Should Watch

- **Volume climaxes:** Extremely high volume on a sharp move often signals exhaustion. If an uptrend produces a massive volume spike on a rally, it may be a blowoff top.
- **Volume divergence:** Price making new highs on declining volume is a warning sign. Demand is drying up.
- **Dark pool prints:** Large block trades executed off-exchange (reported via TRF data) can indicate institutional accumulation or distribution. Several services track these.

## 3. Sector Analysis

Markets do not move uniformly. At any given time, some sectors are leading and others are lagging. Understanding sector dynamics helps swing traders pick the right stocks and time their entries.

### 3.1 Sector Rotation Theory

Sector rotation is the movement of money between sectors based on the economic cycle. Sam Stovall's framework (building on the business cycle) identifies which sectors outperform in each phase:

| Economic Phase | Characteristics | Leading Sectors | Lagging Sectors |
|---------------|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Early Expansion** | Recovery from recession; low rates; rising consumer confidence | Consumer Discretionary, Financials, Industrials, Real Estate | Utilities, Consumer Staples, Healthcare |
| **Mid Expansion** | Strong growth; rising earnings; moderate inflation | Technology, Communication Services, Materials | Utilities, Consumer Staples |
| **Late Expansion** | Peak growth; rising inflation; tightening monetary policy | Energy, Materials, Industrials | Technology, Consumer Discretionary |
| **Contraction** | Declining growth; falling earnings; easing monetary policy | Utilities, Consumer Staples, Healthcare | Energy, Financials, Consumer Discretionary |

**How to use this as a swing trader:**
1. Identify the current economic phase using ISM PMI, yield curve shape, and Fed policy stance.
2. Overweight your swing trades toward the leading sectors for that phase.
3. Avoid or short the lagging sectors.
4. Sector rotation often anticipates the economic cycle by 3-6 months, so the stock market leads the economy.

### 3.2 Relative Strength Comparison

Relative strength (RS) compares the performance of one asset against another (typically its sector against the S&P 500, or a stock against its sector). This is distinct from the RSI indicator.

#### Calculating Relative Strength

```
RS Line = Price of Stock / Price of Benchmark (e.g., S&P 500)
```

- **Rising RS line:** Stock/sector is outperforming the benchmark.
- **Falling RS line:** Stock/sector is underperforming the benchmark.
- **RS breakout:** When the RS line breaks to a new high before price does, it is a leading indicator of outperformance.

#### Practical RS Ranking System

| RS Percentile | Interpretation | Action |
|--------------|----------------|--------|
| 90-100 | Top relative performers | Primary long candidates; leaders in a bull market |
| 70-89 | Above-average performers | Secondary long candidates |
| 30-69 | Average performers | Avoid; no edge |
| 10-29 | Below-average performers | Potential short candidates in a bear market |
| 0-9 | Worst relative performers | Primary short candidates; avoid for longs |

**IBD (Investor's Business Daily) RS Rating** is a popular commercial implementation, ranking stocks 1-99 based on 12-month price performance weighted toward the most recent quarter. Stocks with RS ratings above 80 are the strongest relative performers.

#### Sector ETF Comparison Table

| Sector | Major ETF | Key Characteristics for Swing Trading |
|--------|----------|--------------------------------------|
| Technology | XLK, QQQ | High beta; leads in bull markets; wide swings |
| Healthcare | XLV | Defensive; lower correlation to market; steady trends |
| Financials | XLF | Rate-sensitive; cyclical; strong in early recovery |
| Consumer Discretionary | XLY | Cyclical; Amazon-heavy; consumer confidence proxy |
| Consumer Staples | XLP | Defensive; low beta; outperforms in bear markets |
| Energy | XLE | Commodity-driven; volatile; late-cycle leader |
| Industrials | XLI | Economic bellwether; cyclical; infrastructure exposure |
| Materials | XLB | Commodity-sensitive; inflation hedge; late-cycle |
| Utilities | XLU | Bond proxy; rate-sensitive; defensive; low volatility |
| Real Estate | XLRE | Rate-sensitive; income-focused; early-cycle beneficiary |
| Communication Services | XLC | Growth + value mix; includes Meta, Google, Netflix |

### 3.3 Inter-Market Relationships

Markets are interconnected. Changes in one asset class often signal moves in another.

| Relationship | Mechanism | Swing Trading Implication |
|-------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Bonds up, Stocks up** | Risk-on; falling yields boost equity valuations | Bullish backdrop for swing longs |
| **Bonds down, Stocks down** | Rising rates + risk-off; tightening financial conditions | Bearish; reduce exposure |
| **Dollar up, Commodities down** | Commodities priced in USD become more expensive for foreign buyers | Avoid commodity-related swings; favor domestic revenue companies |
| **Dollar down, Commodities up** | Weak dollar boosts commodity demand | Favor energy, materials, mining stocks |
| **Yield curve steepening** | Economy strengthening; banks profit from wider spread | Bullish for financials, cyclicals |
| **Yield curve inverting** | Recession signal (historically 12-18 months ahead) | Shift to defensive sectors; reduce overall risk |
| **Copper up, Gold down** | Economic confidence rising (copper = growth, gold = fear) | Risk-on; favor cyclicals |
| **Copper down, Gold up** | Economic concern rising | Risk-off; favor defensives |
| **High-yield credit spreads widening** | Credit stress; risk aversion increasing | Major warning sign; reduce all long exposure |
| **High-yield credit spreads narrowing** | Credit confidence; risk appetite strong | Bullish signal; increase long exposure |

**Key inter-market assets to monitor:**

- **TLT** (20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF): Falling TLT = rising rates = headwind for growth stocks
- **DXY** (Dollar Index): Strong dollar headwind for multinationals and emerging markets
- **HYG** (High Yield Corporate Bond ETF): Falling HYG = credit stress = risk-off
- **GLD** (Gold ETF): Rising gold = increasing uncertainty / inflation fears
- **/CL** (Crude Oil Futures): Energy sector driver; inflation input
- **FXI** (China Large-Cap ETF): Global growth proxy

## 4. Timing Factors

Not all days, weeks, and months are created equal. Statistical patterns in market behavior can give swing traders an edge in timing entries and exits.

### 4.1 Seasonality

#### Monthly Seasonality (S&P 500, historical averages 1950-2024)

| Month | Avg Return | Win Rate | Notes |
|-------|-----------|----------|-------|
| January | +1.0% | 62% | "January Effect" / "January Barometer"; small-caps often outperform |
| February | +0.1% | 53% | Weak; often gives back January gains |
| March | +1.0% | 65% | Quarter-end rebalancing can support prices |
| April | +1.3% | 70% | Historically one of the strongest months |
| May | +0.2% | 58% | "Sell in May and go away" starts |
| June | +0.1% | 53% | Weak; summer doldrums begin |
| July | +1.0% | 60% | Mid-summer rally; strong first half of the month |
| August | -0.1% | 52% | Weakest summer month; vacation/low volume |
| September | -0.5% | 45% | Historically the worst month; mutual fund tax-loss selling |
| October | +0.8% | 60% | "Bear killer"; often marks bottoms; volatile |
| November | +1.5% | 68% | Start of the strongest period; post-election year strong |
| December | +1.3% | 73% | "Santa Claus rally" (last 5 trading days + first 2 of January); tax-loss selling pressure early in month |

**The Best Six Months:** November through April historically produces the majority of annual stock market gains. This is the "Sell in May" anomaly documented by the Stock Trader's Almanac.

#### Key Seasonal Patterns

- **Santa Claus Rally:** Last 5 trading days of December + first 2 of January. Historically positive ~75% of the time. If it fails, it is considered a warning sign for the year ahead.
- **January Barometer:** "As January goes, so goes the year." When January is positive, the full year has been positive ~85% of the time.
- **Pre-holiday rallies:** The 1-2 trading days before major holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas, 4th of July) tend to be bullish.
- **October bottoming pattern:** Major bear market lows have disproportionately occurred in October (1987, 2002, 2008, 2022).

### 4.2 Day-of-Week Effects

| Day | Historical Tendency | Notes |
|-----|-------------------|-------|
| **Monday** | Weakest day on average | "Monday effect"; weekend risk processing; gap-downs more common |
| **Tuesday** | Often reverses Monday's direction | Turnaround Tuesday is a known pattern after strong Monday selloffs |
| **Wednesday** | Neutral to slightly positive | Mid-week; FOMC announcements often on Wednesday |
| **Thursday** | Slightly positive | Pre-Friday positioning |
| **Friday** | Positive bias historically | Short-covering before the weekend; portfolio window dressing |

**Practical use:** Avoid entering new longs on Monday opens. Tuesday often provides better entry prices, especially after a Monday selloff. Friday afternoon is generally a poor time to initiate new short positions due to short-covering dynamics.

### 4.3 Options Expiration Effects

Options expiration dates create mechanical flows that influence stock prices.

| Event | Timing | Market Effect |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| **Monthly OpEx** | 3rd Friday of each month | Increased volume; "max pain" magnet effect; pin risk near strikes |
| **Quarterly OpEx ("Quad Witching")** | 3rd Friday of March, June, September, December | Stock options, index options, stock futures, and index futures all expire; extremely high volume; volatile |
| **0DTE Options** | Daily | Intraday gamma effects; exaggerated moves near key strikes; new phenomenon |
| **LEAPS Expiration** | January (3rd Friday) | Long-term options expire; can create unusual hedging flows |

**Max Pain Theory:** Options market makers are positioned such that the stock settles at the price where the maximum number of options contracts expire worthless. This creates a gravitational pull toward certain strike prices in the days before expiration.

**Gamma Exposure (GEX):** When market makers are short options (common), their delta hedging creates a feedback loop:
- **Positive GEX environment:** Market makers buy dips and sell rallies (dampens volatility). Common when VIX is low.
- **Negative GEX environment:** Market makers sell into selloffs and buy into rallies (amplifies volatility). Common during sharp declines.

**Swing trading implication:** The week of monthly OpEx tends to be range-bound as max pain exerts its pull. The week after OpEx often sees larger directional moves as the hedging "pin" is released.

### 4.4 Earnings Season

Approximately four times per year, the majority of companies report quarterly earnings. This creates a predictable cycle of volatility.

| Earnings Period | Approximate Dates | Notes |
|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| Q4 Earnings | Mid-January to mid-February | Accompanied by annual guidance; most impactful |
| Q1 Earnings | Mid-April to mid-May | Tax season overlap |
| Q2 Earnings | Mid-July to mid-August | Summer; lower volume amplifies moves |
| Q3 Earnings | Mid-October to mid-November | Often coincides with October volatility |

**Swing trading rules around earnings:**
1. **Never hold a swing position through earnings** unless it is a deliberate earnings play with a predefined risk plan. The average stock moves 5-8% on earnings; this exceeds normal swing stop-losses.
2. **Post-earnings drift:** Stocks that beat estimates significantly tend to continue drifting higher for 1-4 weeks after the report. Stocks that miss badly continue to drift lower. This is a well-documented anomaly.
3. **Earnings gap plays:** A gap up on strong earnings with heavy volume that holds above the prior day's high is a bullish continuation signal. Enter on the first pullback.
4. **Pre-earnings run-up:** Stocks often rally 3-5 days before their report on anticipation. This is a low-risk swing entry if you sell before the actual announcement.

### 4.5 FOMC and Economic Calendar

Federal Reserve meetings and major economic releases are the most significant scheduled catalysts for the broad market.

#### FOMC Calendar

- **8 meetings per year** (roughly every 6 weeks).
- The statement is released at 2:00 PM ET, followed by the press conference at 2:30 PM ET.
- **Pre-FOMC drift:** Research shows the S&P 500 has a positive bias in the 24 hours before the FOMC announcement. This is one of the most robust anomalies in market microstructure.
- **Post-FOMC reversal:** The initial reaction to the FOMC decision often reverses within 1-3 trading days. Avoid chasing the first move.
- **Dot plot meetings** (March, June, September, December) include updated economic projections and are more impactful than interim meetings.

#### Key Economic Releases

| Release | Frequency | Typical Impact | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| **Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP)** | Monthly (1st Friday) | High | Most-watched employment data; sets tone for the month |
| **CPI (Consumer Price Index)** | Monthly | Very High | Inflation gauge; directly impacts Fed policy expectations |
| **PPI (Producer Price Index)** | Monthly | Moderate | Leading indicator for CPI |
| **ISM PMI** | Monthly | High | Above 50 = expansion; below 50 = contraction |
| **GDP** | Quarterly | High | Backward-looking but market-moving if surprise |
| **Retail Sales** | Monthly | Moderate | Consumer spending gauge |
| **Initial Jobless Claims** | Weekly (Thursday) | Low-Moderate | High-frequency labor market indicator |
| **PCE Price Index** | Monthly | High | Fed's preferred inflation measure |
| **Consumer Confidence** | Monthly | Moderate | Forward-looking sentiment |

**Practical approach:** Check the economic calendar every Sunday night. If a major release (CPI, NFP, FOMC) falls during the week, plan swing entries for after the event, not before. The market often consolidates in the 1-2 days ahead of a major release and then moves sharply on the data.

## 5. Sentiment Indicators

Sentiment indicators measure the psychological state of market participants. They are most useful as contrarian signals at extremes.

### 5.1 VIX (CBOE Volatility Index)

Covered in Section 1.3 above. In the sentiment context:

- **VIX > 30:** Extreme fear. Historically, this has been a better time to buy than sell (contrarian signal).
- **VIX < 12:** Extreme complacency. Often precedes volatility expansion and market pullbacks.
- **VIX spike + VIX close well off its highs (long upper wick on VIX candle):** Suggests fear was absorbed and often marks a short-term market low.

### 5.2 Put/Call Ratio

The put/call ratio measures the volume of put options traded relative to call options.

| Ratio (CBOE Equity P/C) | Interpretation | Signal |
|--------------------------|---------------|--------|
| > 1.0 | Heavy put buying; extreme fear | Contrarian bullish; market near a low |
| 0.7 - 1.0 | Elevated hedging; cautious | Neutral to slightly bullish |
| 0.5 - 0.7 | Normal range | No signal |
| < 0.5 | Heavy call buying; extreme greed | Contrarian bearish; market near a top |

**Best use:** Look at the 5-day or 10-day moving average of the equity put/call ratio rather than the daily reading, which is noisy. Readings above 0.9 (10-day MA) have historically preceded rallies within 2-4 weeks.

### 5.3 AAII Sentiment Survey

The American Association of Individual Investors surveys its members weekly on whether they are bullish, bearish, or neutral on the stock market over the next six months.

| Reading | Interpretation | Historical Outcome |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Bullish > 55% | Extreme optimism | S&P 500 below-average returns over next 6 months |
| Bullish < 25% | Extreme pessimism | S&P 500 above-average returns over next 6 months |
| Bull-Bear Spread > +30 | Euphoria | Contrarian sell signal |
| Bull-Bear Spread < -20 | Despondency | Contrarian buy signal |

**Caveat:** AAII sentiment is a better signal at extremes than in the middle range. It is also better as a multi-week trend than a single-week reading. Several consecutive weeks of extreme bearishness is a stronger signal than one outlier week.

### 5.4 CNN Fear & Greed Index

A composite index combining seven indicators into a single 0-100 score:

1. Stock price momentum (S&P 500 vs. 125-day MA)
2. Stock price strength (52-week highs vs. lows on NYSE)
3. Stock price breadth (McClellan Volume Summation Index)
4. Put/Call ratio
5. Market volatility (VIX)
6. Safe haven demand (stock vs. bond returns)
7. Junk bond demand (yield spread)

| Score | Label | Swing Implication |
|-------|-------|------------------|
| 0-25 | Extreme Fear | Contrarian buy zone; look for swing long setups |
| 25-45 | Fear | Cautiously accumulating |
| 45-55 | Neutral | No edge from sentiment |
| 55-75 | Greed | Cautious; tighten stops on longs |
| 75-100 | Extreme Greed | Contrarian sell zone; avoid new longs; consider shorts |

### 5.5 Short Interest Data

Short interest is the total number of shares sold short and not yet covered.

| Metric | What It Measures | Signal |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| **Short Interest Ratio (Days to Cover)** | Short shares / average daily volume | > 5 days: heavily shorted; potential short squeeze fuel |
| **Short % of Float** | Short shares / floating shares | > 20%: very heavily shorted |
| **Changes in Short Interest** | Bi-weekly updates (FINRA) | Rising short interest = increasing bearish bets; declining = shorts covering |

**Short squeeze potential:** When a heavily shorted stock (> 20% of float) breaks above resistance on high volume, forced short-covering can accelerate the move dramatically. This creates explosive swing trade opportunities but also extreme volatility.

**Contrarian use:** Aggregate short interest at extreme highs often coincides with market bottoms (everyone who wants to short has already done so). At extreme lows, it suggests complacency.

### 5.6 Insider Trading Data

Corporate insiders (officers, directors, 10%+ holders) must report their trades to the SEC (Form 4 filings).

| Activity | Interpretation | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| **Cluster buying** (3+ insiders buying within 30 days) | Strong bullish signal | Insiders buy for one reason: they expect the price to rise |
| **Single insider buy** | Mildly bullish | More meaningful if it is a large purchase relative to their holdings |
| **Insider selling** | Ambiguous | Insiders sell for many reasons (diversification, taxes, personal needs); less useful as a signal |
| **Cluster selling** | Moderately bearish | Multiple insiders selling at the same time is more concerning |

**Practical use:** Screen for stocks with recent insider cluster buys that are also setting up technically. The combination of insider buying + a bullish chart pattern has a strong historical track record.

## 6. Fundamental Screening

While swing trading is primarily technical, fundamental screening helps identify which stocks are most likely to sustain strong trends.

### 6.1 Earnings and Revenue Growth

Stocks with accelerating earnings and revenue growth tend to produce the strongest price trends. This is the cornerstone of the CAN SLIM methodology developed by William O'Neil.

| Metric | What to Look For | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **EPS Growth (Quarterly YoY)** | > 25% growth vs. same quarter last year | Accelerating earnings drive institutional buying |
| **EPS Growth (Annual)** | > 20% growth over each of the past 3 years | Consistent growers attract long-term capital |
| **Revenue Growth (Quarterly YoY)** | > 20% growth | Revenue growth validates earnings growth (not just cost-cutting) |
| **Earnings Acceleration** | Each quarter's growth rate exceeding the prior quarter | The most bullish fundamental signal |
| **Earnings Surprise** | Beat estimates by > 5% | Positive surprises lead to estimate revisions and price drift |
| **Analyst Estimate Revisions** | Upward revisions to forward EPS estimates | Forward-looking; drives institutional buying |

**Swing trading filter:** Require at least 20% quarterly EPS growth and 15% revenue growth as a baseline screen. This eliminates value traps and weak fundamentals.

### 6.2 Relative Strength Rankings

As discussed in Section 3.2, relative price strength is one of the most robust factors in stock selection.

**Screening criteria for swing longs:**
- RS Rating > 80 (top 20% of all stocks by price performance)
- Price above the 50-day SMA and the 200-day SMA
- 50-day SMA above the 200-day SMA (golden cross in place)
- Industry group rank in the top 40% (don't fight a weak sector)

**Screening criteria for swing shorts:**
- RS Rating < 20 (bottom 20%)
- Price below the 50-day SMA and the 200-day SMA
- 50-day SMA below the 200-day SMA (death cross in place)
- Industry group rank in the bottom 40%

### 6.3 Float and Share Structure

The float (shares available for public trading) directly impacts price behavior.

| Float Size | Characteristics | Swing Trading Notes |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Micro-float (< 10M shares)** | Explosive moves; low liquidity; wide spreads | Very high reward/risk; use limit orders; position size small |
| **Small float (10-50M shares)** | Strong moves on volume; moderate liquidity | Good for aggressive swing traders; watch for gaps |
| **Medium float (50-200M shares)** | Smoother trends; good liquidity | Ideal for most swing strategies |
| **Large float (> 200M shares)** | Slow, steady moves; very liquid | Good for conservative swing traders; smaller % moves |

**Float turnover ratio:** Average daily volume / float. A ratio above 0.1 (10% of float trading daily) indicates intense interest and potential for sustained moves.

### 6.4 Institutional Ownership

Institutional investors (mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds) drive the majority of market volume.

| Metric | What to Look For | Why |
|--------|-----------------|-----|
| **Institutional Ownership %** | 20-70% | Too low = no institutional interest; too high = overcrowded |
| **Number of Institutional Holders** | Increasing quarter-over-quarter | Rising institutional interest = accumulation |
| **Top Holder Quality** | Funds with strong performance records owning the stock | Smart money backing |
| **13F Filings** | Quarterly reports of institutional holdings | Shows what institutions are buying/selling (with a delay) |

**Ideal fundamental profile for a swing long candidate:**

| Factor | Ideal Criteria |
|--------|---------------|
| Quarterly EPS Growth | > 25% YoY |
| Revenue Growth | > 15% YoY |
| RS Rating | > 80 |
| Institutional Ownership | 20-65% and increasing |
| Float | 20-150M shares |
| Industry Group Rank | Top 30% |
| Analyst Estimates | Upward revisions in last 30 days |

## 7. Putting It All Together: A Market Conditions Checklist

Before taking any swing trade, assess the broader market conditions using this checklist:

### Daily Market Assessment

| Factor | Check | Current Status |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| **S&P 500 Trend** | Price vs. 20/50/200 SMA; HH/HL or LH/LL structure | Bull / Bear / Sideways |
| **ADX** | Trending or range-bound? | Trending (>25) / Ranging (<20) |
| **VIX** | Current level and direction | Low (<15) / Normal (15-20) / Elevated (>20) |
| **Breadth** | NYSE advance/decline line; % of stocks above 50 SMA | Confirming / Diverging |
| **Sector Leadership** | Which sectors are leading/lagging? | Offensive / Defensive |
| **Fear & Greed** | CNN Index reading | Extreme Fear / Neutral / Extreme Greed |
| **Key Events** | FOMC, CPI, earnings this week? | Clear / Event risk |

### Position Sizing Based on Conditions

| Condition | Suggested Exposure |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Strong bull trend, low VIX, broad participation | 75-100% invested; full position sizes |
| Bull trend, moderate VIX, mixed breadth | 50-75% invested; standard positions |
| Sideways/uncertain, elevated VIX | 25-50% invested; reduced positions |
| Bear trend, high VIX, narrow breadth | 0-25% invested; mostly cash or short |
| Extreme fear spike (VIX > 35) in a bull context | Contrarian opportunity; selective buying |
