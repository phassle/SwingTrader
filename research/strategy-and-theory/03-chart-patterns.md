# Part 1: Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are formations created by price movement on a chart over weeks to months. They represent the battle between buyers and sellers and, when correctly identified, offer high-probability trade setups with clearly defined entry, stop-loss, and target levels.

---

## 1. Head and Shoulders

### Description
The Head and Shoulders (H&S) is one of the most well-known and reliable reversal patterns in technical analysis. It signals a transition from a bullish trend to a bearish trend. The inverse (Inverse Head and Shoulders) signals the opposite -- a reversal from bearish to bullish.

### Structure
- **Left Shoulder:** Price rallies to a peak, then declines.
- **Head:** Price rallies again to a higher peak, then declines back to approximately the same support level (the "neckline").
- **Right Shoulder:** Price rallies a third time but fails to reach the height of the head, then declines again toward the neckline.
- **Neckline:** The support line connecting the lows between the left shoulder/head and head/right shoulder. Can be horizontal or slightly sloped.

### How to Identify
1. There must be a preceding uptrend (for standard H&S) or downtrend (for inverse).
2. The head must be the highest point (or lowest for inverse).
3. The two shoulders should be roughly symmetrical in height and width, though perfect symmetry is not required.
4. The neckline should be identifiable by connecting the two reaction lows (or highs for inverse).
5. Volume typically decreases from left shoulder to head to right shoulder. A volume spike on the neckline break adds confirmation.

### Reliability Rating
**High (8/10).** Thomas Bulkowski's research shows the H&S pattern has a failure rate of approximately 4-5% when the neckline is broken, making it one of the most reliable chart patterns. The inverse H&S has a slightly higher failure rate around 6%.

### Measured Move / Target
The target is calculated by measuring the vertical distance from the top of the head to the neckline, then projecting that distance downward (or upward for inverse) from the neckline breakout point.

- **Example:** If the head peaks at $50 and the neckline is at $42, the measured distance is $8. The target after a neckline break at $42 would be $34.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume:** A surge in volume on the neckline break is critical confirmation. Low-volume breakdowns are more prone to failure.
- **Retest:** Price often retests the broken neckline from below (now acting as resistance). This retest offers a second entry opportunity.
- **RSI divergence:** Bearish divergence on the RSI between the head and right shoulder adds conviction.
- **MACD crossover:** A bearish MACD crossover during right shoulder formation supports the pattern.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** Short (or sell long position) on a decisive close below the neckline with above-average volume, or on the retest of the neckline.
- **Stop-loss:** Above the right shoulder. A tighter stop can be placed just above the neckline.
- **Target:** The measured move target. Many traders take partial profits at 50% of the measured move and trail the remainder.
- **Timeframe:** The pattern is most reliable on daily and weekly charts. Formation typically takes 1-6 months.
- **Tip:** The longer the pattern takes to form, the more significant the eventual breakdown.

### Inverse Head and Shoulders
Everything mirrors the standard pattern. It forms at the bottom of a downtrend and signals a bullish reversal. The entry is a break above the neckline with volume confirmation. The measured move is projected upward. Inverse H&S patterns are statistically slightly less reliable than the standard version but remain among the best reversal patterns for swing trading.

## 2. Double Top / Double Bottom

### Description
The Double Top is a bearish reversal pattern that forms after an extended uptrend. Price reaches a high, pulls back, rallies to approximately the same high, and then declines. The Double Bottom is the bullish mirror, forming after a downtrend.

### Structure
- **Double Top:** Two peaks at roughly the same price level, separated by a trough (the "valley" or "confirmation line").
- **Double Bottom:** Two troughs at roughly the same price level, separated by a peak.
- The confirmation/trigger line connects the intermediate low (double top) or high (double bottom).

### How to Identify
1. A preceding trend must exist (uptrend for double top, downtrend for double bottom).
2. The two peaks/troughs should be within 1-3% of each other in price. They do not need to be exactly equal.
3. The time between the two peaks/troughs is typically 2-7 weeks. Too close together and it may just be consolidation; too far apart and the pattern loses relevance.
4. Volume is usually higher on the first peak/trough and lower on the second, indicating waning momentum.
5. The pattern completes only when price breaks through the confirmation line.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** Double tops have a failure rate around 11-17% according to Bulkowski's research. Double bottoms are slightly more reliable at around 11% failure. The key differentiator is volume on the breakout.

### Measured Move / Target
Measure the vertical distance from the peaks to the confirmation line, then project that distance from the breakout point.

- **Example (Double Top):** Peaks at $60, confirmation line at $54. Distance = $6. Target = $54 - $6 = $48.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume surge** on the break of the confirmation line.
- **Momentum divergence:** RSI or MACD failing to reach the same level on the second peak/trough.
- **Retest:** Price often retests the confirmation line after the breakout.
- **Candlestick patterns** at the second peak/trough (e.g., bearish engulfing at a double top).

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** On a close beyond the confirmation line with volume, or on the retest.
- **Stop-loss:** Above the second peak (double top) or below the second trough (double bottom).
- **Target:** The measured move. Consider scaling out in thirds.
- **Tip:** The "Adam and Eve" variant (one sharp peak + one rounded peak) is considered more reliable by some practitioners. Watch for higher timeframe support/resistance alignment for added confidence.

## 3. Cup and Handle

### Description
The Cup and Handle is a bullish continuation pattern identified by William O'Neil. It resembles a tea cup when viewed on a chart. Price forms a rounded bottom (the cup), followed by a small downward drift or consolidation (the handle), and then breaks out to the upside.

### Structure
- **Cup:** A U-shaped (not V-shaped) rounding bottom. The depth of the cup should ideally be 12-35% of the prior advance. The cup typically takes 7-65 weeks to form.
- **Handle:** A small pullback or flag on the right side of the cup, typically retracing 1/3 to 1/2 of the cup's depth. Should last at least 1-2 weeks. The handle should form in the upper half of the cup.
- **Breakout:** Price breaks above the resistance line drawn across the top of the cup (the rim).

### How to Identify
1. A prior uptrend must exist (this is a continuation pattern).
2. The cup should have a smooth, rounded bottom -- not a sharp V-bottom.
3. Both sides of the cup should reach approximately the same height.
4. The handle should drift downward, not upward. An upward-drifting handle is a warning sign.
5. Volume should be low during the handle and increase sharply on the breakout above the rim.

### Reliability Rating
**High (8/10).** When properly formed with volume confirmation, the cup and handle is one of the most reliable continuation patterns. O'Neil considered it a primary pattern for identifying growth stocks. Failure rate is around 5% with proper criteria.

### Measured Move / Target
Measure the depth of the cup (from rim to bottom) and project that distance upward from the breakout point.

- **Example:** Rim at $50, cup bottom at $40. Depth = $10. Target = $50 + $10 = $60.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume:** Must increase significantly on the breakout. This is non-negotiable for this pattern.
- **Handle volume:** Should be light/declining, indicating a lack of selling pressure.
- **Relative strength:** The stock should show relative strength compared to the broader market during cup formation.
- **Moving averages:** Price should be above the 50-day and 200-day moving averages at the breakout point for the strongest setups.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** On a breakout above the rim of the cup with volume at least 50% above the 50-day average volume. Some traders buy during the handle formation anticipating the breakout.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the low of the handle. A wider stop can be placed at the midpoint of the cup.
- **Target:** The measured move. Strong stocks often exceed the measured move significantly.
- **Tip:** Look for cup and handle patterns in stocks with strong fundamentals (earnings growth, revenue acceleration) as identified in the CAN SLIM methodology. The best cup and handle breakouts occur during healthy market uptrends.

## 4. Ascending Triangle

### Description
The Ascending Triangle is a bullish continuation pattern (though it can occasionally appear as a reversal pattern at bottoms). It is characterized by a flat or horizontal resistance level at the top and a rising support trendline connecting higher lows.

### Structure
- **Flat resistance:** Multiple price touches at approximately the same horizontal level, indicating a supply zone.
- **Rising support:** A trendline connecting at least two (preferably three) progressively higher lows, indicating increasing buying pressure.
- **Breakout:** Price eventually breaks above the flat resistance as buyers overpower the supply.

### How to Identify
1. The horizontal resistance line should have at least 2-3 clear touches.
2. The ascending support line should have at least 2-3 clear touches with higher lows.
3. Price should oscillate between these two lines, with the range narrowing over time.
4. Volume generally decreases as the pattern develops, then surges on the breakout.
5. The pattern typically takes 1-3 months to form.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** Ascending triangles break upward approximately 64-68% of the time according to Bulkowski's research. The failure rate after an upward breakout is around 11%. Downward breakouts from ascending triangles (which do occur) tend to be less reliable.

### Measured Move / Target
Measure the height of the triangle at its widest point (the distance from the flat resistance to the lowest point of the ascending line) and project that upward from the breakout point.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume expansion** on the breakout is essential.
- **Breakout timing:** The strongest breakouts typically occur between 50-75% of the way through the triangle (measured from left to apex). Breakouts near the apex tend to be weaker.
- **Retest:** Price often retests the former resistance (now support) before continuing higher.
- **Trend context:** Ascending triangles that form during broader uptrends have higher success rates.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** Buy on a close above the resistance line with strong volume, or on the retest.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the most recent higher low, or below the ascending trendline.
- **Target:** The measured move. Extend targets if the broader trend is strongly bullish.
- **Tip:** Watch for false breakouts -- a single intraday poke above resistance that closes back below is not a valid breakout. Wait for a closing confirmation.

## 5. Descending Triangle

### Description
The Descending Triangle is the bearish counterpart to the ascending triangle. It features a flat or horizontal support level at the bottom and a descending resistance trendline connecting lower highs.

### Structure
- **Flat support:** Multiple price touches at approximately the same horizontal level.
- **Descending resistance:** A trendline connecting at least two or three lower highs, indicating increasing selling pressure.
- **Breakdown:** Price eventually breaks below the flat support as sellers overpower the demand.

### How to Identify
1. The horizontal support should have at least 2-3 clear touches.
2. The descending trendline should connect 2-3 progressively lower highs.
3. Volume should generally contract as the pattern develops.
4. The pattern typically forms over 1-3 months.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** Descending triangles break downward approximately 64% of the time. However, when they break upward (which happens about 36% of the time), the upward move can be explosive, so traders should not automatically assume a bearish outcome.

### Measured Move / Target
Same method as ascending triangle: measure the widest point of the triangle and project it downward from the breakdown point.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume increase** on the breakdown.
- **A close below the support line** (not just an intraday wick).
- **Sector/market weakness** aligning with the pattern increases reliability.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** Short on a close below the support line with volume, or on the retest of the broken support.
- **Stop-loss:** Above the most recent lower high, or above the descending trendline.
- **Target:** The measured move.
- **Tip:** Be cautious about shorting descending triangles during strong bull markets. The market context matters significantly.

## 6. Symmetrical Triangle

### Description
The Symmetrical Triangle is a neutral pattern formed by converging trendlines -- a descending upper trendline and an ascending lower trendline. It indicates a period of consolidation and indecision, with a breakout in either direction possible. It slightly favors continuation of the prior trend.

### Structure
- **Converging trendlines:** One descending (lower highs) and one ascending (higher lows).
- **Narrowing price range:** The range tightens progressively as the apex approaches.
- **Breakout:** Eventually price breaks decisively through one of the trendlines.

### How to Identify
1. Both trendlines should have at least two touches each (three is better).
2. The trendlines should converge at roughly equal angles, forming a symmetrical shape.
3. Price oscillates between the lines with decreasing amplitude.
4. Volume typically declines throughout the formation.
5. The pattern should have an identifiable prior trend.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate (6/10).** Symmetrical triangles are less directionally reliable than ascending or descending triangles because they can break either way. The prior trend provides a slight edge (approximately 54% continue in the direction of the prior trend). Once the breakout occurs, the measured move success rate is moderate.

### Measured Move / Target
Measure the height of the triangle at its base (the widest point) and project from the breakout point.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume surge on breakout** is critical due to the pattern's neutral nature.
- **Breakout timing:** Best between 50-75% of the distance from base to apex.
- **Follow-through:** Look for 2-3 consecutive closes beyond the trendline.
- **Avoid breakouts near the apex** -- these tend to be unreliable and produce choppy action.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** Wait for a confirmed breakout with volume before taking a directional position. Some aggressive traders trade within the triangle, buying at support and selling at resistance.
- **Stop-loss:** On the opposite side of the triangle from your entry.
- **Target:** The measured move.
- **Tip:** Because the direction is uncertain, consider using a straddle-like approach by placing buy-stops above and sell-stops below the trendlines, though be wary of whipsaws.

## 7. Flags and Pennants

### Description
Flags and Pennants are short-term continuation patterns that form after a sharp, steep price move (the "flagpole"). They represent a brief pause or consolidation before the trend resumes. They are among the most reliable short-term patterns for swing traders.

### Structure

**Flag:**
- A rectangular shape that slopes against the prior trend direction.
- Formed by two parallel trendlines containing the consolidation.
- A bullish flag slopes downward; a bearish flag slopes upward.

**Pennant:**
- A small symmetrical triangle that forms after a sharp move.
- Converging trendlines with decreasing volatility.
- Similar to a symmetrical triangle but much shorter in duration.

**Both:**
- The flagpole is the sharp move preceding the pattern.
- Duration is typically 1-4 weeks (shorter than most chart patterns).

### How to Identify
1. There must be a sharp, nearly vertical prior move (the flagpole) -- ideally on high volume.
2. The consolidation (flag or pennant) should have declining or low volume.
3. The flag should contain the price action within roughly parallel lines.
4. The pennant should show converging trendlines.
5. The consolidation should be relatively brief (1-4 weeks).
6. The breakout should be in the direction of the prior trend with a volume surge.

### Reliability Rating
**High (8/10).** Flags and pennants are among the most reliable continuation patterns when they form after a clear, strong trending move. Bulkowski reports that bullish flags meet their target about 64% of the time, while bearish flags meet theirs about 55% of the time. Pennants have similar statistics.

### Measured Move / Target
The target is typically equal to the length of the flagpole, projected from the breakout point of the flag/pennant.

- **Example:** Stock surges from $30 to $40 (flagpole = $10), forms a flag pulling back to $37, then breaks out. Target = $37 + $10 = $47.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume contraction** during the flag/pennant formation.
- **Volume expansion** on the breakout.
- **The flagpole must be clearly defined** -- no flagpole, no flag.
- **Tight consolidation** (shallow retracement of the flagpole) indicates strong conviction.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** On the breakout from the flag/pennant with volume confirmation. Some aggressive traders enter within the flag near the support line.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the lowest point of the flag (bullish) or above the highest point (bearish).
- **Target:** The flagpole projection. Many traders find that the second leg (post-flag) moves faster than the first.
- **Tip:** Flags that retrace less than 38.2% of the flagpole are considered "tight" and tend to produce the most powerful breakouts. Flags lasting more than 4-5 weeks may morph into a different pattern.

## 8. Wedges (Rising and Falling)

### Description
Wedges are patterns formed by two converging trendlines that both slope in the same direction. Unlike triangles (where the lines slope in opposite directions), both lines in a wedge slope either upward or downward. Wedges typically signal reversals.

### Rising Wedge (Bearish)

**Structure:**
- Both the support line and resistance line slope upward.
- The resistance line is less steep than the support line, causing the lines to converge.
- Price makes higher highs and higher lows, but the range narrows.

**Identification:**
1. Both trendlines slope upward with at least 2-3 touches each.
2. Volume typically diminishes as the pattern progresses.
3. The pattern can appear after an uptrend (reversal) or during a downtrend (continuation).
4. Duration: typically 3-6 months.

### Falling Wedge (Bullish)

**Structure:**
- Both the support line and resistance line slope downward.
- The support line is steeper than the resistance line.
- Price makes lower highs and lower lows, but the range narrows.

**Identification:**
1. Both trendlines slope downward with at least 2-3 touches each.
2. Volume decreases during formation.
3. Can appear after a downtrend (reversal) or during an uptrend (continuation).

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** Rising wedges break downward approximately 68% of the time. Falling wedges break upward approximately 68% of the time. When the breakout occurs in the expected direction, the pattern is fairly reliable.

### Measured Move / Target
Measure the height of the wedge at its widest point (the back/left side) and project from the breakout point. Some traders target a retracement to the beginning of the wedge (the starting point of the pattern).

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume spike on breakout.**
- **RSI divergence:** Bearish divergence in a rising wedge, bullish divergence in a falling wedge.
- **Break of the lower trendline** (rising wedge) or upper trendline (falling wedge) with a closing confirmation.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** On a decisive break of the trendline opposite to the slope direction (lower line for rising wedge, upper line for falling wedge).
- **Stop-loss:** Above the most recent high (rising wedge) or below the most recent low (falling wedge).
- **Target:** The widest point of the wedge projected from the breakout, or the starting level of the pattern.
- **Tip:** Rising wedges that form after an extended uptrend tend to produce especially sharp selloffs because they represent the final "exhaustion" phase of the trend.

## 9. Channels

### Description
Channels are patterns where price moves between two parallel trendlines over an extended period. They provide both trend direction information and clear levels for entry and exit.

### Ascending Channel (Bullish)
- **Structure:** Two parallel upward-sloping trendlines containing price action.
- **Lower line (support):** Connects the series of higher lows.
- **Upper line (resistance):** Connects the series of higher highs.
- **Behavior:** Price bounces between the two lines, trending higher overall.

### Descending Channel (Bearish)
- **Structure:** Two parallel downward-sloping trendlines.
- **Upper line (resistance):** Connects lower highs.
- **Lower line (support):** Connects lower lows.
- **Behavior:** Price bounces between the two lines, trending lower overall.

### Horizontal Channel (Neutral / Range)
- **Structure:** Two horizontal parallel lines.
- **Effectively a trading range or rectangle** (see section 10).

### How to Identify
1. Each trendline should have at least 2-3 clear touches (more is better).
2. The lines should be roughly parallel. Non-parallel lines may indicate a wedge.
3. Price should oscillate relatively cleanly between the two lines.
4. Volume may vary but often increases at the trendline touches.
5. The channel is valid until price breaks decisively through one of the lines.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate (6/10) for channel continuation; High (7-8/10) for trading within the channel.** Channels eventually break, and predicting when they break is challenging. However, trading within the channel (buying at support, selling at resistance) can be highly reliable while the pattern persists.

### Measured Move / Target
- **Within-channel trades:** Target the opposite channel line.
- **Breakout trades:** The measured move equals the width (height) of the channel, projected from the breakout point.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Multiple touches:** More touches on each line increase confidence in the channel.
- **Volume:** Watch for volume climaxes at channel boundaries as potential reversal or breakout signals.
- **Momentum divergence:** RSI divergence at the upper or lower channel line may signal an impending breakout.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Within channel:** Buy near support (lower line), sell near resistance (upper line). Use tight stops just outside the channel.
- **Breakout trade:** Enter when price breaks and closes outside the channel with volume. Target the channel width projected from the breakout.
- **Stop-loss:** For channel-bound trades, just outside the trendline. For breakout trades, back inside the channel.
- **Tip:** The "median line" (midpoint of the channel) often acts as an intra-channel support/resistance level. Partial profits can be taken there. Draw the channel on the daily chart but use the 4-hour or 1-hour chart for precise entries.

## 10. Rectangles / Ranges

### Description
A Rectangle (also called a trading range or consolidation zone) is a pattern where price moves sideways between a horizontal support level and a horizontal resistance level. It represents equilibrium between buyers and sellers and can resolve with a breakout in either direction, though it slightly favors continuation.

### Structure
- **Horizontal resistance:** A clearly defined ceiling tested multiple times.
- **Horizontal support:** A clearly defined floor tested multiple times.
- **Price oscillation:** Price bounces between these levels with no clear upward or downward bias.

### How to Identify
1. At least two touches on both the support and resistance levels.
2. The range should be wide enough to trade (at least a few percent between support and resistance).
3. Volume often declines during the range and then surges on the breakout.
4. The pattern can last weeks to months.
5. More touches on the boundaries generally mean a more powerful eventual breakout.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate (6/10) for breakout direction; High (8/10) for range-bound trading.** Predicting the breakout direction is difficult, but trading within the range is highly effective as long as the boundaries hold. Bulkowski reports that rectangles continue the prior trend about 55% of the time.

### Measured Move / Target
The height of the rectangle (distance from support to resistance) is projected from the breakout point.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume surge on breakout.**
- **A close beyond the range boundary** (not just a wick).
- **Retest of the broken boundary** (former support becomes resistance, or vice versa).
- **Increasing volume on tests of the boundary that eventually breaks** may signal accumulation/distribution.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Range trading:** Buy near support with a stop below it; sell/short near resistance with a stop above it. This approach has a high win rate while the range persists.
- **Breakout trading:** Enter on a decisive break with volume. Target the measured move.
- **Stop-loss:** For range trades, just outside the boundary. For breakout trades, back inside the range.
- **Tip:** Watch Bollinger Band width -- when the bands narrow significantly within a range, a breakout is often imminent. The longer the rectangle persists, the more powerful the eventual breakout tends to be.

# Part 2: Candlestick Patterns

Candlestick patterns are formations created by one or more candlesticks (typically 1-3) that provide short-term trading signals. They are most effective when they occur at key support/resistance levels, trendlines, or in conjunction with chart patterns.

**Important note on candlestick reliability:** Individual candlestick patterns are statistically less reliable than chart patterns. Their power increases dramatically when combined with other technical factors (support/resistance, volume, trend context, indicators). Never trade a candlestick pattern in isolation.

---

## 11. Doji Variations

### General Description
A Doji is a candlestick where the open and close are virtually equal (or very close), creating a very thin or nonexistent real body. It signals indecision between buyers and sellers and is often a precursor to a reversal, especially after a strong trend.

### Standard Doji
- **Appearance:** A thin cross or plus sign with roughly equal upper and lower shadows.
- **Meaning:** Pure indecision. Neither buyers nor sellers gained control.
- **Context:** After an uptrend, it warns of potential reversal. After a downtrend, it warns of potential bottom. In a range, it has less significance.

### Dragonfly Doji
- **Appearance:** A T-shape. Long lower shadow, no (or very small) upper shadow. Open and close are at or near the high.
- **Meaning:** Sellers pushed price significantly lower during the session, but buyers rallied and pushed it back to the open. Strong buying pressure at lower levels.
- **Context:** Bullish at the bottom of a downtrend. At the top of an uptrend, it can still be bullish but is less reliable.

### Gravestone Doji
- **Appearance:** An inverted T-shape. Long upper shadow, no (or very small) lower shadow. Open and close are at or near the low.
- **Meaning:** Buyers pushed price significantly higher, but sellers drove it back down to the open. Strong selling pressure at higher levels.
- **Context:** Bearish at the top of an uptrend. At the bottom of a downtrend, it has less significance.

### Long-Legged Doji
- **Appearance:** Very long upper and lower shadows with the open/close near the middle. Looks like a large plus sign.
- **Meaning:** Extreme indecision with significant volatility. Both sides fought hard but neither won.
- **Context:** Often marks a significant turning point. The longer the legs, the more significant the indecision.

### Reliability Rating
**Low-Moderate (4/10) alone; Moderate-High (7/10) with confirmation.** A doji by itself is just a signal of indecision. Its predictive power comes entirely from context (where it appears) and confirmation (what follows).

### Confirmation Signals
- **Follow-through candle:** A strong bearish candle after a doji at a top, or a strong bullish candle after a doji at a bottom, confirms the reversal.
- **Volume:** High volume on the doji day adds significance.
- **Location:** Dojis at major support/resistance levels or trendlines are far more meaningful.
- **Gap:** A doji that gaps away from the prior candle (doji star) is more significant.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Do not trade the doji alone.** Wait for confirmation.
- **After an uptrend doji:** If the next candle closes below the doji's low, consider a short or exit long positions.
- **After a downtrend doji:** If the next candle closes above the doji's high, consider a long or cover short positions.
- **Tip:** Multiple dojis in succession indicate a market in deep indecision -- a big move is often imminent, but direction is uncertain.

## 12. Hammer and Inverted Hammer

### Hammer

**Description:** A single-candle bullish reversal pattern that appears at the bottom of a downtrend. It has a small real body at the upper end of the range and a long lower shadow (at least twice the length of the body).

**Appearance:**
- Small real body (can be green or red, though green is slightly more bullish).
- Long lower shadow (2x or more the body length).
- Little or no upper shadow.
- Occurs after a downtrend.

**Meaning:** Sellers pushed price significantly lower during the session, but buyers stepped in aggressively and pushed price back near the open. It shows that buying interest is emerging at lower prices.

### Inverted Hammer

**Description:** A single-candle pattern that appears at the bottom of a downtrend. It has a small body at the lower end and a long upper shadow.

**Appearance:**
- Small real body at the bottom.
- Long upper shadow (2x or more the body length).
- Little or no lower shadow.
- Occurs after a downtrend.

**Meaning:** Buyers attempted a rally during the session but were pushed back by sellers. However, the fact that buyers are trying signals potential reversal. Less intuitively bullish than the hammer, but still a reversal signal with confirmation.

### Reliability Rating
- **Hammer: Moderate-High (7/10).** One of the more reliable single-candle reversal patterns.
- **Inverted Hammer: Moderate (5/10).** Requires stronger confirmation than the hammer.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Next-day candle:** A strong bullish candle following the hammer/inverted hammer confirms the reversal.
- **Gap up:** If the next candle opens above the hammer's body, it adds strong confirmation.
- **Volume:** High volume on the hammer day indicates strong buying absorption.
- **Support level:** A hammer at a known support level or key moving average is much more significant.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** After confirmation -- buy when the next candle closes above the hammer's high.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the low of the hammer's shadow.
- **Target:** The nearest resistance level or a risk/reward-based target (minimum 2:1).
- **Tip:** A "hammer" that appears in the middle of a range or in an uptrend is not a hammer pattern. Context is essential. The same candle shape in an uptrend is called a "Hanging Man" and is actually bearish.

## 13. Engulfing Patterns

### Description
Engulfing patterns are two-candle reversal patterns where the second candle's body completely "engulfs" (covers the entire range of) the first candle's body. They are among the most powerful candlestick reversal signals.

### Bullish Engulfing
- **First candle:** A bearish (red) candle within a downtrend.
- **Second candle:** A bullish (green) candle whose body completely engulfs the first candle's body (opens below the first candle's close and closes above the first candle's open).
- **Meaning:** A dramatic shift in momentum from sellers to buyers.

### Bearish Engulfing
- **First candle:** A bullish (green) candle within an uptrend.
- **Second candle:** A bearish (red) candle whose body completely engulfs the first candle's body (opens above the first candle's close and closes below the first candle's open).
- **Meaning:** A dramatic shift in momentum from buyers to sellers.

### How to Identify
1. There must be a clear prior trend.
2. The second candle's real body must completely contain the first candle's real body. (Shadows do not need to be engulfed, only bodies.)
3. The colors should be opposite -- bearish then bullish (for bullish engulfing) or bullish then bearish (for bearish engulfing).
4. The larger the second candle relative to the first, the stronger the signal.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** Engulfing patterns are among the most reliable two-candle reversal patterns. Bullish engulfing patterns at major support levels have a particularly strong track record. The pattern is significantly more reliable when the engulfing candle has above-average volume.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume:** The engulfing candle should have higher volume than the preceding candle.
- **Follow-through:** The candle after the engulfing pattern should continue in the reversal direction.
- **Size:** A larger engulfing candle relative to surrounding candles adds power.
- **Location:** At key support/resistance levels, trendlines, or moving averages.
- **Third-candle confirmation:** Some conservative traders wait for a third candle to close beyond the engulfing candle's body.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** At the close of the engulfing candle (aggressive) or after the next candle confirms (conservative).
- **Stop-loss:** Below the low of the bullish engulfing pattern or above the high of the bearish engulfing.
- **Target:** The next major support/resistance level or a 2:1 to 3:1 reward-to-risk ratio.
- **Tip:** An engulfing pattern that also engulfs several prior candles (not just one) is an exceptionally strong signal. These "outside days" represent major momentum shifts.

## 14. Morning Star / Evening Star

### Description
Three-candle reversal patterns. The Morning Star is bullish (appears at the bottom of a downtrend), and the Evening Star is bearish (appears at the top of an uptrend). They are considered among the most reliable candlestick reversal patterns.

### Morning Star (Bullish)
1. **First candle:** A long bearish candle, confirming the downtrend is intact.
2. **Second candle:** A small-bodied candle (can be bullish or bearish, including a doji) that gaps down from the first candle. This is the "star" representing indecision.
3. **Third candle:** A long bullish candle that closes well into the body of the first candle (ideally above the midpoint). This confirms the reversal.

### Evening Star (Bearish)
1. **First candle:** A long bullish candle, confirming the uptrend.
2. **Second candle:** A small-bodied candle that gaps up from the first candle.
3. **Third candle:** A long bearish candle that closes well into the body of the first candle.

### How to Identify
1. Clear prior trend.
2. The gap between the first and second candles adds reliability but is not strictly required in all markets (crypto and forex often trade 24/7 and do not gap as frequently as stocks).
3. The smaller the body of the second candle, the better. A doji star is the strongest variation ("Morning Doji Star" or "Evening Doji Star").
4. The third candle should close at least past the midpoint of the first candle's body.

### Reliability Rating
**High (8/10).** Morning and Evening Star patterns are among the most reliable candlestick formations, especially when they occur at established support/resistance levels with volume confirmation. The doji star variant is even more reliable.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume:** Ideally, volume is high on the first candle, low on the star, and high again on the third candle.
- **Gap:** The presence of gaps between the star and the surrounding candles adds significant reliability.
- **Third candle strength:** The further the third candle closes into the first candle's body, the stronger the signal.
- **Follow-through:** The candle after the three-candle pattern should continue in the reversal direction.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** At the close of the third candle or on the open of the fourth candle.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the low of the star candle (morning star) or above the high of the star candle (evening star).
- **Target:** The nearest major support/resistance level or a 2:1+ reward-to-risk ratio.
- **Tip:** Morning/Evening stars that form at the 200-day moving average, at major Fibonacci retracement levels, or at prior pivot points are the highest-probability setups.

## 15. Three White Soldiers / Three Black Crows

### Description
Three-candle continuation/reversal patterns representing strong directional conviction.

### Three White Soldiers (Bullish)
- Three consecutive long-bodied bullish candles.
- Each candle opens within (or near) the body of the previous candle.
- Each candle closes progressively higher.
- Small or no upper shadows (indicating buyers maintained control through the close).
- Appears after a downtrend or a base, signaling strong buying pressure.

### Three Black Crows (Bearish)
- Three consecutive long-bodied bearish candles.
- Each candle opens within (or near) the body of the previous candle.
- Each candle closes progressively lower.
- Small or no lower shadows.
- Appears after an uptrend, signaling strong selling pressure.

### How to Identify
1. All three candles should have long bodies relative to surrounding candles.
2. Each successive candle should close at a new high (soldiers) or low (crows).
3. Each candle should open within the body of the preceding candle (not above the prior close for soldiers or below the prior close for crows -- this would indicate exhaustion gaps).
4. Shadows should be small, indicating sustained pressure throughout each session.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10).** These patterns show strong conviction but can sometimes appear at the end of a move (exhaustion) rather than the beginning. The key differentiator is whether the candles show diminishing or consistent size. Three candles of decreasing size may signal weakening momentum.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Consistent candle size:** All three candles should be of similar size. If the third candle is significantly smaller, momentum may be fading.
- **Volume:** Volume should increase or remain strong throughout the three candles.
- **Context:** More reliable at the start of a new trend (after a reversal pattern) than after an extended move.
- **RSI:** If RSI is not yet overbought (soldiers) or oversold (crows), the pattern has more room to run.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** After the third candle closes, enter in the direction of the pattern.
- **Stop-loss:** Below the low of the first soldier or above the high of the first crow.
- **Target:** The next major resistance (soldiers) or support (crows) level.
- **Tip:** Be cautious if the third candle is the one that pushes RSI into overbought/oversold territory -- a pullback is likely before continuation. Consider waiting for a pullback entry.

## 16. Harami Patterns

### Description
A two-candle reversal pattern where the second candle's body is completely contained within the body of the first candle. "Harami" is Japanese for "pregnant." It is the opposite of the engulfing pattern.

### Bullish Harami
- **First candle:** A long bearish candle in a downtrend.
- **Second candle:** A small bullish candle whose body is entirely within the first candle's body.
- **Meaning:** The selling momentum has stalled. The small body shows indecision after a strong sell candle.

### Bearish Harami
- **First candle:** A long bullish candle in an uptrend.
- **Second candle:** A small bearish candle whose body is entirely within the first candle's body.
- **Meaning:** The buying momentum has stalled.

### Harami Cross
A more powerful variant where the second candle is a doji (instead of a small-bodied candle). The Harami Cross is considered significantly more reliable than the standard Harami.

### How to Identify
1. A clear prior trend.
2. The first candle should have a long body.
3. The second candle's body must be completely contained within the first candle's body.
4. The second candle should be relatively small compared to the first.
5. Colors should ideally be opposite, though same-colored haramis are still valid.

### Reliability Rating
**Low-Moderate (5/10) for standard; Moderate (6/10) for Harami Cross.** The Harami is one of the weaker reversal patterns when used alone. It is best used as a warning signal that the current trend is losing momentum, followed by waiting for additional confirmation.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Third candle:** A strong candle in the reversal direction following the Harami confirms the signal.
- **Volume:** Low volume on the second candle (Harami body) supports the indecision interpretation.
- **Key levels:** Harami patterns at major support/resistance are more meaningful.
- **Multiple Haramis:** Consecutive Harami-like candles (inside bars) indicate building pressure for a significant move.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** After confirmation from the next candle, not on the Harami itself.
- **Stop-loss:** Beyond the extreme of the first (large) candle.
- **Target:** The next support/resistance level.
- **Tip:** The Harami is best used as a "heads up" signal to tighten stops or reduce exposure rather than as a primary entry signal. In a strong trend, Haramis are frequently just pauses before continuation, not actual reversals.

## 17. Shooting Star

### Description
A single-candle bearish reversal pattern that appears at the top of an uptrend. It has a small real body at the lower end of the range and a long upper shadow (at least twice the body length). It is essentially the same shape as an inverted hammer but appearing at a different location (top versus bottom).

### Appearance
- Small real body near the low of the candle (can be red or green, though red is slightly more bearish).
- Long upper shadow (at least 2x the body length).
- Little or no lower shadow.
- Must appear after an uptrend.

### Meaning
Buyers pushed price significantly higher during the session, but sellers took over and drove price back down near the open. It shows that supply is overcoming demand at higher prices.

### How to Identify
1. Must follow an established uptrend or a strong rally.
2. The candle should gap up from the prior candle (adds significance but not strictly required).
3. The upper shadow should be at least twice the length of the real body.
4. There should be little to no lower shadow.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate (6/10).** The Shooting Star is a decent warning signal but frequently fails without confirmation. It is more reliable when occurring at known resistance levels or with extremely high volume (indicating a blow-off top).

### Confirmation Signals
- **Bearish follow-through:** The next candle should close below the shooting star's body.
- **Volume:** High volume on the shooting star day suggests distribution.
- **Resistance level:** Much more meaningful at key resistance zones.
- **Gap down:** If the next candle gaps down, the signal is strongly confirmed.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Entry:** Short after the next candle confirms by closing below the shooting star's body/low.
- **Stop-loss:** Above the high of the shooting star (the top of the upper shadow).
- **Target:** The nearest support level or a 2:1 reward-to-risk ratio.
- **Tip:** A shooting star with exceptionally high volume and at a multi-month or multi-year resistance level is one of the strongest single-candle bearish signals in technical analysis.

## 18. Spinning Top

### Description
A candle with a small real body and long upper and lower shadows of roughly equal length. It indicates indecision, similar to a doji, but with a slightly larger body. The color of the body is considered unimportant.

### Appearance
- Small real body (can be green or red).
- Both upper and lower shadows are long, roughly equal in length.
- The body is approximately centered between the shadows.

### Meaning
Both buyers and sellers were active during the session (hence the long shadows), but neither was able to establish dominance (hence the small body). It represents market indecision and uncertainty about future direction.

### How to Identify
1. The real body should be small relative to the total candle range.
2. Both shadows should be present and significant.
3. The pattern is most meaningful after a strong trend.

### Reliability Rating
**Low (3/10) alone; Moderate (5-6/10) with context.** Like the doji, the spinning top is primarily a warning signal that the current trend may be losing steam. It requires additional confirmation and context to be actionable.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Trend context:** After a prolonged trend, a spinning top signals potential exhaustion.
- **Cluster:** Multiple spinning tops/dojis in succession strongly suggest indecision and an impending significant move.
- **Follow-through:** The direction of the candle following the spinning top determines the short-term bias.

### Swing Trading Application
- **As a warning signal:** Tighten stops on existing positions when spinning tops appear after a prolonged trend.
- **As a setup:** Wait for the next candle to establish direction, then enter accordingly.
- **Tip:** Spinning tops are more useful as exit signals (for existing positions) than as entry signals. They tell you to be cautious but do not tell you which direction to trade.

## 19. Marubozu

### Description
A Marubozu is a candle with a long body and no (or extremely small) shadows. It represents total dominance by either buyers or sellers during the session. "Marubozu" is Japanese for "bald" or "shaved" (referring to the absence of shadows).

### Bullish Marubozu (White/Green)
- Long green body.
- Opens at (or very near) the low and closes at (or very near) the high.
- No significant shadows.
- **Meaning:** Buyers were in complete control from open to close. Extremely bullish.

### Bearish Marubozu (Black/Red)
- Long red body.
- Opens at (or very near) the high and closes at (or very near) the low.
- No significant shadows.
- **Meaning:** Sellers were in complete control from open to close. Extremely bearish.

### Variations
- **Full Marubozu:** Absolutely no shadows. Rare.
- **Opening Marubozu:** No shadow on the opening side (lower for bullish, upper for bearish). A small shadow exists on the closing side.
- **Closing Marubozu:** No shadow on the closing side (upper for bullish, lower for bearish). A small shadow exists on the opening side. The closing marubozu is considered slightly more significant because the close represents the final consensus.

### How to Identify
1. The real body should be significantly longer than average candles.
2. Shadows should be absent or negligible (less than 5-10% of the body length).
3. Context matters -- a Marubozu is more significant at key levels or at the start of a move.

### Reliability Rating
**Moderate-High (7/10) as a continuation signal; Moderate (6/10) as a reversal signal.** A Marubozu shows extreme conviction. When appearing at the beginning of a trend or after a breakout, it strongly supports continuation. When appearing after an extended move, it can signal a climax/exhaustion (potential reversal), though this is harder to trade.

### Confirmation Signals
- **Volume:** High volume on the Marubozu day confirms strong conviction.
- **Breakout context:** A Marubozu that breaks through a key level (resistance for bullish, support for bearish) is a very strong signal.
- **Follow-through:** The next candle should ideally continue in the Marubozu's direction.
- **Exhaustion warning:** If a Marubozu appears after an extended move and is accompanied by extremely high volume, it may be a climax top or bottom. Look for reversal signals in the following sessions.

### Swing Trading Application
- **Trend continuation entry:** When a Marubozu appears early in a trend or at a breakout, enter in the direction of the Marubozu with a stop at the midpoint of the candle (aggressive) or at the opposite end (conservative).
- **Breakout confirmation:** A Marubozu through a resistance/support level is one of the strongest possible breakout confirmations.
- **Caution after extended trends:** A Marubozu at the end of a long trend, especially with a volume spike, may be an exhaustion candle. Do not chase.
- **Tip:** Multiple consecutive Marubozu candles are extremely rare but powerful. If you see two or three in a row, you are witnessing strong institutional conviction.

# Part 3: Pattern Combination Strategies for Swing Traders

The most effective swing trading setups combine chart patterns with candlestick patterns. Here are the highest-probability combinations:

## Top Pattern Combinations

| Setup | Chart Pattern | Candlestick Trigger | Reliability |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Neckline Reversal | Head & Shoulders neckline break | Bearish engulfing or evening star at right shoulder | Very High |
| Double Bottom Entry | Double bottom at support | Hammer or morning star at second low | Very High |
| Breakout Confirmation | Cup & handle / triangle breakout | Marubozu or bullish engulfing on breakout day | Very High |
| Channel Bounce | Ascending channel support touch | Hammer or bullish harami at lower trendline | High |
| Flag Entry | Bull flag pullback | Morning star or hammer at flag support | High |
| Range Reversal | Rectangle support/resistance | Engulfing pattern at range boundary | High |
| Wedge Break | Falling wedge upper line break | Three white soldiers beginning the breakout | High |

## General Rules for Combining Patterns

1. **Trend alignment:** The most reliable setups align the candlestick signal with the broader trend direction. Counter-trend candlestick signals require much stronger confirmation.

2. **Volume is the ultimate filter:** Any pattern -- chart or candlestick -- is significantly more reliable when accompanied by above-average volume. Volume confirms that institutional traders (who move markets) are participating.

3. **Multiple timeframe confirmation:** A candlestick reversal on the daily chart that coincides with a chart pattern breakout on the weekly chart is a very high-probability setup.

4. **Support/resistance confluence:** Patterns that form at levels where multiple support/resistance factors converge (horizontal S/R, moving averages, Fibonacci levels, trendlines) are the highest-probability setups.

5. **Risk management remains paramount:** Even the best pattern combinations fail 20-30% of the time. Always use stop-losses and position sizing to limit risk to 1-2% of trading capital per trade.

# Key Takeaways for Swing Traders

1. **Chart patterns provide the framework; candlestick patterns provide the trigger.** Use chart patterns to identify the setup (where price is in relation to structure) and candlestick patterns to time the entry.

2. **Always trade with the trend.** Bullish patterns in uptrends and bearish patterns in downtrends have significantly higher success rates than counter-trend patterns.

3. **Volume confirms everything.** Breakouts, reversals, and continuation patterns all require volume confirmation. Low-volume signals are unreliable.

4. **Measured moves are guidelines, not guarantees.** They provide reasonable price targets, but always be prepared for patterns to under-perform or over-shoot.

5. **The best patterns are obvious.** If you have to squint to see the pattern, it probably is not there. Clear, well-formed patterns attract more attention from other traders, creating self-fulfilling prophecy dynamics.

6. **Risk management is non-negotiable.** No pattern works every time. Keep losses small (1-2% of capital) and let winners run to the measured move target or beyond.

7. **Practice pattern recognition.** Study historical charts extensively. The ability to rapidly recognize patterns in real-time comes from experience, not just knowledge.

8. **Beware of pattern failure.** A failed pattern often produces a strong move in the opposite direction. A failed head and shoulders (price reclaims the neckline) is a powerful bullish signal. Always have a plan for what to do if the pattern fails.
