# Swing Trading Fundamentals

## What is Swing Trading?

Swing trading is a style of trading that attempts to capture short- to medium-term gains in a financial instrument over a period of a few days to several weeks. Swing traders primarily use technical analysis to identify trading opportunities, though some also incorporate fundamental analysis.

### Core Definition
- **Timeframe:** Typically 2 days to 6 weeks (most commonly 2-10 trading days)
- **Goal:** Capture a single "swing" or move within a larger trend
- **Approach:** Enter positions at swing lows and exit at swing highs (or vice versa for shorts)
- **Analysis:** Primarily technical, with optional fundamental screening

### How It Differs from Other Styles

| Aspect | Day Trading | Swing Trading | Position Trading |
|--------|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Holding Period | Minutes to hours | Days to weeks | Weeks to months/years |
| Trades per Month | 20-100+ | 5-15 | 1-5 |
| Time Commitment | Full-time (6-8 hrs/day) | Part-time (1-2 hrs/day) | Minimal (weekly review) |
| Capital Needed | $25,000+ (PDT rule in US) | $5,000-$25,000 | $10,000+ |
| Overnight Risk | None | Yes | Yes |
| Profit Target/Trade | 0.5-2% | 3-10% | 10-50%+ |
| Stop-Loss/Trade | 0.1-0.5% | 1-3% | 5-15% |
| Stress Level | Very high | Moderate | Low |
| Best For | Full-time traders | Working professionals | Long-term investors |

## History and Evolution

### Origins
- **Charles Dow (1900s):** Dow Theory laid the groundwork by identifying primary trends, secondary trends, and minor fluctuations. Swing trading targets secondary trends (corrections within primary trends).
- **R.N. Elliott (1930s):** Elliott Wave Theory introduced the concept of market waves and corrective patterns, forming a basis for timing swings.
- **W.D. Gann (1930s-1940s):** Developed geometric and time-cycle analysis to identify turning points.

### Modern Development
- **1950s-1970s:** Technical analysis formalized with indicators (RSI, MACD, Stochastic) that became the backbone of swing trading.
- **1990s:** Online brokerages democratized access. Retail traders could now swing trade with reasonable commissions.
- **2000s:** Commission-free trading (Robinhood, etc.), advanced charting platforms (TradingView, thinkorswim), and algorithmic screening tools made swing trading accessible to everyone.
- **2020s:** AI-driven pattern recognition, social sentiment analysis, and automated alert systems have added new layers to swing trading strategies.

## Key Principles and Philosophy

### 1. Trade with the Trend
- "The trend is your friend" - always identify the primary trend before entering
- Use multiple timeframes: weekly for trend direction, daily for entry signals
- Never fight a strong trend; look for pullback entries in the trend direction

### 2. Risk Management First
- Never risk more than 1-2% of total capital on a single trade
- Always define stop-loss BEFORE entering a trade
- Position size should be calculated from the stop-loss distance

### 3. Let Winners Run, Cut Losers Short
- Use trailing stops to protect profits as a trade moves in your favor
- Exit losers quickly when they hit the stop-loss - no hoping or averaging down
- Aim for a minimum 1:2 risk/reward ratio (risk $1 to make $2)

### 4. Plan the Trade, Trade the Plan
- Every trade should have: entry price, stop-loss, take-profit target
- Write down the rationale before entering
- Review and journal every trade after closing

### 5. Patience and Discipline
- Wait for high-probability setups; don't force trades
- Not trading is a valid position
- Avoid revenge trading after a loss

### 6. Focus on Expectancy, Not Win Rate
- A 40% win rate with 1:3 risk/reward is highly profitable
- Expectancy = (Win Rate × Avg Win) - (Loss Rate × Avg Loss)
- Positive expectancy over many trades is what matters

### 7. Multi-Timeframe Analysis
- Higher timeframe (weekly) sets the bias (bullish/bearish)
- Trading timeframe (daily) identifies entry setups
- Lower timeframe (4h/1h) fine-tunes entry and exit points

## Typical Holding Periods

### Short Swing (2-5 Days)
- Targets quick moves within established trends
- Often uses 4-hour and daily charts
- Tighter stops, smaller targets (3-5%)
- Higher frequency, requires more monitoring

### Standard Swing (1-3 Weeks)
- The most common swing trading timeframe
- Uses daily charts primarily, weekly for context
- Moderate stops (5-8%), moderate targets (8-15%)
- Good balance of effort and reward

### Extended Swing (3-6 Weeks)
- Captures larger moves and trend continuations
- Uses daily and weekly charts
- Wider stops (8-15%), larger targets (15-30%+)
- Lower frequency, less monitoring needed
- Blurs into position trading territory

## Risk/Reward Ratios

### Common Ratios Used

| R:R Ratio | Required Win Rate (Breakeven) | Description |
|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| 1:1 | 50% | Barely profitable after costs |
| 1:1.5 | 40% | Minimum acceptable for most traders |
| 1:2 | 33% | Standard swing trading target |
| 1:3 | 25% | Excellent; allows low win rate |
| 1:4 | 20% | Trend-following with tight stops |
| 1:5+ | 17% | Rare but very powerful when achieved |

### Practical Guidelines
- **Minimum R:R:** 1:2 (risk $1 to make at least $2)
- **Ideal R:R:** 1:3 or better
- **Average swing trader win rate:** 40-55%
- **Target expectancy:** $0.20-$0.50 per dollar risked

## Capital Requirements

### Minimum Starting Capital by Market

| Market | Minimum Recommended | Ideal Starting Capital |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| US Stocks (no margin) | $5,000 | $15,000-$25,000 |
| US Stocks (with PDT rule) | $25,000 | $30,000-$50,000 |
| Forex | $1,000 (with leverage) | $5,000-$10,000 |
| Crypto | $1,000 | $5,000-$10,000 |
| Futures | $10,000 | $25,000-$50,000 |
| Options | $5,000 | $10,000-$25,000 |

### Position Sizing Basics
- **1% Rule:** Risk no more than 1% of account per trade
- Example: $25,000 account → max risk per trade = $250
- If stop-loss is $2.50 below entry → position size = 100 shares
- Formula: `Position Size = (Account × Risk%) / (Entry - Stop Loss)`

## Pros and Cons

### Advantages
- **Time-efficient:** Only requires 1-2 hours per day (evenings work fine)
- **Compatible with full-time job:** Can analyze and place orders outside market hours
- **Captures larger moves:** Higher profit per trade compared to day trading
- **Lower transaction costs:** Fewer trades mean less commission and slippage
- **Less stressful:** No need to watch every tick
- **Lower capital requirement:** No PDT rule if holding overnight (in US)
- **Compounding potential:** Consistent 3-5% per trade compounds significantly

### Disadvantages
- **Overnight/weekend risk:** Gaps can blow through stop-losses
- **Slower feedback loop:** Fewer trades make it harder to learn quickly
- **Patience required:** Waiting for setups can be boring
- **Opportunity cost:** Capital is tied up for days/weeks
- **Market regime dependent:** Choppy markets can whipsaw swing traders
- **Emotional challenges:** Watching unrealized profits fluctuate requires discipline
- **Gap risk:** Earnings, news events, or weekend events can cause adverse gaps

## Common Markets for Swing Trading

### Stocks (Equities)
- **Best for:** Most swing traders; widest selection of instruments
- **Advantages:** Deep liquidity, extensive research available, many screener tools
- **Key considerations:** PDT rule in US ($25K minimum for frequent trading), earnings risk, sector rotation
- **Ideal stocks:** Mid-cap to large-cap, average daily volume > 500K, beta 1.0-2.0, clear trending behavior

### Forex (Foreign Exchange)
- **Best for:** Traders who want 24/5 market access and leverage
- **Advantages:** 24-hour market (Sunday-Friday), high liquidity, low spreads on major pairs
- **Key considerations:** Leverage amplifies risk, correlation between pairs, central bank events
- **Best pairs for swing:** EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, AUD/USD

### Cryptocurrency
- **Best for:** Traders comfortable with high volatility, 24/7 markets
- **Advantages:** 24/7 trading, high volatility = larger swings, no PDT rule
- **Key considerations:** Extreme volatility, regulatory uncertainty, exchange risk, lower liquidity on altcoins
- **Best for swing:** BTC, ETH, SOL, and top-20 coins by market cap

### Commodities and Futures
- **Best for:** Experienced traders seeking diversification
- **Advantages:** Leverage, diversification from equities, clear trending behavior
- **Key considerations:** Contract expiration, margin requirements, limit moves
- **Popular instruments:** Gold, crude oil, natural gas, S&P 500 futures (ES)

### ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)
- **Best for:** Swing traders wanting sector or index exposure with lower single-stock risk
- **Advantages:** Diversified exposure, high liquidity, options available
- **Popular ETFs:** SPY, QQQ, IWM, XLF, XLE, XLK, GLD, TLT
- **Leveraged ETFs:** TQQQ, SOXL, UPRO (higher risk, suitable for short-term swings only)

## Glossary of Key Terms

- **Swing High:** A peak formed when price makes a higher high then reverses
- **Swing Low:** A trough formed when price makes a lower low then reverses
- **Pullback:** A temporary reversal within a larger trend
- **Breakout:** Price moving beyond a defined support/resistance level
- **Consolidation:** A period of sideways price action before the next move
- **Support:** A price level where buying pressure tends to prevent further decline
- **Resistance:** A price level where selling pressure tends to prevent further advance
- **Stop-Loss:** A predetermined price level to exit a losing trade
- **Take-Profit:** A predetermined price level to exit a winning trade
- **Risk/Reward Ratio:** The potential profit compared to potential loss on a trade
- **Position Sizing:** Determining how many shares/contracts to trade based on risk tolerance
- **Gap:** A price jump between one session's close and the next session's open

## Application Design Takeaways

When building a swing trading application, consider these requirements derived from fundamentals:

1. **Multi-timeframe chart display** - Weekly, daily, and intraday views
2. **Screening/scanning engine** - Filter stocks by volume, price range, trend, volatility
3. **Risk calculator** - Position sizing based on account size, risk percentage, and stop distance
4. **Trade journal** - Log entries, exits, rationale, screenshots, and P&L
5. **Alert system** - Notify when price reaches entry, stop, or target levels
6. **Backtesting module** - Test strategies against historical data
7. **Performance dashboard** - Track win rate, R:R, expectancy, drawdown over time
8. **Watchlist management** - Organize stocks by setup type, sector, or status
