# Candlestick Examples And Scenarios For Swing Trading

Prepared by Codex on 2026-03-08. This file complements `11-candlestick-interpretation.md` with practical examples of how candlesticks can be interpreted in real swing-trading situations.

## Purpose

The goal here is not to add more pattern names. The goal is to show how the same candle can mean different things depending on:

- trend
- level
- volume
- prior candles
- the next candle

Each scenario below follows the same structure:

1. Market condition
2. What the candle shows
3. Interpretation
4. What confirms it
5. What invalidates it

## Scenario 1: Hammer at clear support after a decline

### Market condition

- The stock has fallen for 5 to 8 days.
- Price is approaching prior daily support.
- The decline is happening with visible fear and extended distance from short moving averages.

### What the candle shows

- Small body near the top of the day's range
- Long lower wick
- Close clearly above the day's low

### Interpretation

This shows that sellers had control at first, but buyers stepped in aggressively and reclaimed price before the close. The candle itself does not say the trend has reversed. It says sellers met resistance at that level.

### What confirms it

- The next candle closes above the hammer's high
- Volume is above average
- The hammer forms exactly at a known support level or gap-fill zone

### What invalidates it

- The next day opens weak and breaks the hammer's low
- The hammer appears in the middle of nowhere without clear support
- The broader weekly trend is strongly bearish and no other support exists

### Practical conclusion

The hammer is a `possible reversal candidate`, not a finished buy signal. For swing trading, the entry is often better only after confirmation above the high.

## Scenario 2: Shooting star directly into resistance

### Market condition

- The stock has risen for several days in a row.
- Price reaches a prior swing high or clear resistance zone.
- The advance is already somewhat extended.

### What the candle shows

- Small body near the lower part of the day's range
- Long upper wick
- Close far below the day's high

### Interpretation

Buyers managed to push price higher but could not keep control. Sellers defended the level and pushed price down before the close.

### What confirms it

- The next candle closes lower
- Volume is elevated
- The candle lines up with a prior top, Fibonacci level, or weekly resistance

### What invalidates it

- The next candle breaks above the shooting star's high
- The rejection appears in a strong trend without any clear level
- Volume is low and the whole move looks illiquid

### Practical conclusion

This is often a better `risk signal` than an automatic short signal. For a long swing trader, it can be a reason to reduce size, move a stop, or avoid a new entry.

## Scenario 3: Bullish engulfing after a washout

### Market condition

- The stock has been weak for several days or weeks.
- A red candle is followed by a larger green candle that engulfs the prior body.
- The situation occurs near support or after an exaggerated decline.

### What the candle shows

- Buyers dominate the prior day's selling pressure
- The shift between sessions is clear

### Interpretation

A bullish engulfing candle shows that the market abruptly changed character. It is especially interesting if the previous move was panicky and the new candle closes strongly.

### What confirms it

- Close above short-term resistance
- Strong volume
- Several following higher lows

### What invalidates it

- The next day price falls back into the lower part of the engulfing candle
- The engulfing appears inside a messy range with no edge
- The whole pattern occurs against a strong weekly downtrend without any clear catalyst

### Practical conclusion

An engulfing candle is often better as a sign that momentum may be shifting than as a standalone entry trigger.

## Scenario 4: Bearish engulfing after an overextended breakout

### Market condition

- The stock first breaks out above a range or prior high
- Many traders chase the breakout
- The same day or the next day, a strong red candle engulfs the prior body

### What the candle shows

- The breakout was not accepted
- Buyers lost the initiative quickly
- Sellers took over aggressively

### Interpretation

This is often a sign of a `failed breakout`, which can be more important than the engulfing name itself.

### What confirms it

- Price falls back below the breakout level
- Volume increases as price moves back into the range
- The next candles show continued weakness

### What invalidates it

- Price immediately regains the breakout level
- The bearish engulfing appears on very low volume
- The range structure is still intact without real weakness

### Practical conclusion

In swing trading, a failed breakout often carries stronger information than a generic bearish candle pattern.

## Scenario 5: Doji after a long trend move

### Market condition

- The stock has moved strongly up or down for several candles
- A doji appears after an extended move

### What the candle shows

- Open and close are close together
- The market failed to continue with the same control into the close

### Interpretation

A doji means hesitation, not reversal by itself. After a strong trend move, it can be the first sign that momentum is fading.

### What confirms it

- The next candle moves in the opposite direction
- Volume is high
- The doji appears at major support or resistance

### What invalidates it

- The next candle strongly continues the trend
- The doji appears in a sideways market
- No level or volume makes the signal relevant

### Practical conclusion

A doji is most useful as a `warning of hesitation`, not as a standalone reversal signal.

## Scenario 6: Several small candles after a strong impulse

### Market condition

- The stock makes a strong impulse up
- That is followed by 2 to 5 small candles with small bodies

### What the candle sequence shows

- The market pauses
- Neither side can force a clear reversal

### Interpretation

This is often more bullish than it looks, as long as price stays near the top of the impulse. Small candles after a strong advance can signal absorption of profit-taking rather than weakness.

### What confirms it

- Break above the consolidation high
- Lower volume during the pullback or consolidation
- Trend and market breadth still support the move

### What invalidates it

- Price falls deeply back into the original impulse
- The consolidation becomes long and loose without edge
- The overall market loses momentum

### Practical conclusion

This is a good example of why single candles are not enough. A sequence of small candles near highs can be strong continuation information.

## Scenario 7: Long upper wick on the breakout day

### Market condition

- The stock breaks above a clear level intraday
- Many traders see the breakout in real time

### What the candle shows

- The day trades well above the breakout level
- But the close is much lower than the high

### Interpretation

A long upper wick on breakout day means price was poorly accepted at higher levels. It is a classic warning sign of a `bull trap`.

### What confirms it

- The next day closes below the breakout level
- Volume is high on the rejection
- The broader market shows risk-off behavior

### What invalidates it

- The next day price stays above the breakout level
- The rejection was small relative to the range
- The breakout is backed by a strong news catalyst and continued volume

### Practical conclusion

When a breakout day leaves a large upper wick, the main focus should be `acceptance above the level`, not the fact that price temporarily traded higher.

## Scenario 8: Long lower wick in a strong uptrend

### Market condition

- The stock is in an established uptrend
- A pullback comes into the 10-day or 20-day moving average
- During the day price is pushed lower but recovers strongly

### What the candle shows

- Buyers defend the trend level
- Selling pressure is absorbed

### Interpretation

This is often continuation, not reversal. In a strong uptrend, a long lower wick near trend support means buyers are still willing to buy dips.

### What confirms it

- The next candle makes a higher high
- The pullback happens on lower volume than the prior advance
- The broader market remains constructive

### What invalidates it

- Price closes below support the next day
- The pullback happens with visible distribution
- The uptrend is already broken on the higher timeframe

### Practical conclusion

The same wick that looks bullish in a downtrend means something different in an uptrend. Context changes the interpretation.

## Scenario 9: Marubozu-like candle after news

### Market condition

- News, earnings, or a strong catalyst hits the market
- A very large candle forms without much opposing wick

### What the candle shows

- One side had almost full control for the whole period
- The market repriced the asset quickly

### Interpretation

This type of candle means strength or weakness, but also elevated risk. After large news-driven candles, the next question is not only direction, but whether price can `hold` the new level.

### What confirms it

- The next day holds above the midpoint of the strong bullish candle
- Volume is exceptional and price consolidates tightly
- The catalyst is genuinely important

### What invalidates it

- The next day retraces the entire move
- There is no follow-through
- The large candle was mostly a short squeeze without a durable catalyst

### Practical conclusion

A large candle after news is not automatically a chase entry. It should be read as `repricing plus elevated risk`.

## Scenario 10: Inside bar or small candle right before expansion

### Market condition

- Volatility has contracted
- A small candle or inside bar forms near an important level

### What the candle shows

- compression
- temporary balance between buyers and sellers

### Interpretation

Small candles are not only hesitation. In the right context, they can signal that the market is loading up for expansion.

### What confirms it

- Break of the inside bar high or low
- Clear volume increase on the expansion
- The pattern sits near a level with structural importance

### What invalidates it

- Continued sideways movement without expansion
- False breakout that immediately returns into the range
- The pattern lacks a clear trend or level

### Practical conclusion

In swing trading, small candles are often most important right before the next large move, not during the move itself.

## Summary decision model

When you see an interesting candlestick, think through this:

1. Does it show control, hesitation, or rejection?
2. Did it appear after a move that makes the signal relevant?
3. Is it at a level where other market participants are likely to react?
4. Is it confirmed by the next price behavior?
5. Does it create a trade where stop, target, and risk/reward are actually reasonable?

If several of those questions do not have a clear answer, the candle is informationally weak even if the pattern name sounds good.

## Bottom line

Good candlestick interpretation is not about recognizing shapes. It is about reading `order flow in context`. A hammer at support after a washout and with confirmation is useful. That same hammer in the middle of a random range is often just noise.
